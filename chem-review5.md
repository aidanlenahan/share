## **Question 1: What type of IMF is formed in the following?**

### a) Freezing water

**Analysis:**
- Water is H₂O, a polar molecule containing O, H, and N
- When water freezes, liquid water molecules must form attractions to solid water
- Water molecules form strong attractions between the hydrogen atom of one molecule and the oxygen atom of another molecule
- This is the definition of a hydrogen bond

**Answer:** $$\boxed{\text{Hydrogen bonds}}$$

---

### b) Liquifying argon

**Analysis:**
- Argon (Ar) is a noble gas with atomic number 18
- Argon is a monatomic element with no polar bonds
- The only attractions that exist between two argon atoms are induced dipole-induced dipole attractions
- These are London dispersion forces (LDF's)

**Answer:** $$\boxed{\text{Induced dipole-induced dipole (London dispersion forces)}}$$

---

### c) Dissolving copper (II) sulfate in water

**Analysis:**
- Copper (II) sulfate is CuSO₄, an ionic compound
- When dissolved in water, the ionic bonds between Cu²⁺ and SO₄²⁻ ions are broken
- Water molecules surround these ions
- The attractions formed are between the polar water molecules and the ions
- This is ion-dipole attraction

**Answer:** $$\boxed{\text{Ion-dipole (also includes induced dipole-induced dipole/LDF's)}}$$

---

### d) Mixing ethanol, C₂H₅OH, with water

**Analysis:**
- Ethanol: C₂H₅OH contains an O-H bond
- Water: H₂O also contains O-H bonds
- Both molecules are polar
- Ethanol's hydrogen (bonded to O) can form hydrogen bonds with water's oxygen
- Additionally, both molecules are polar, so dipole-dipole attractions exist
- London dispersion forces are always present

**Answer:** $$\boxed{\text{Hydrogen bonds, dipole-dipole, and London dispersion forces}}$$

---

### e) Oxygen gas dissolved in water

**Analysis:**
- O₂ is a nonpolar diatomic molecule (two identical atoms)
- Water is polar
- When a nonpolar molecule dissolves in polar water, the induced dipole in O₂ is induced by the dipole in water
- This creates dipole-induced dipole attractions
- London dispersion forces also exist between all particles

**Answer:** $$\boxed{\text{Dipole-induced dipole and London dispersion forces}}$$

## **Question 3: Vapor Pressure Problem**

### Part a) Mass of water that can evaporate

**Given Information:**
- Temperature: 99°C
- Equilibrium vapor pressure: 733.2 mmHg
- Container volume: 2.0 L
- Need to find: mass of water that can evaporate

**Step 1: Convert pressure to atmospheres**
$$P = \frac{733.2 \text{ mmHg}}{760 \text{ mmHg/atm}} = 0.9652 \text{ atm}$$

**Step 2: Convert temperature to Kelvin**
$$T = 99 + 273.15 = 372.15 \text{ K}$$

**Step 3: Use ideal gas law to find moles of water vapor**
$$PV = nRT$$

$$n = \frac{PV}{RT} = \frac{(0.9652 \text{ atm})(2.0 \text{ L})}{(0.08206 \text{ L·atm/mol·K})(372.15 \text{ K})}$$

$$n = \frac{1.9304}{30.52} = 0.0632 \text{ mol}$$

**Step 4: Convert moles to grams using molar mass of water (18.02 g/mol)**
$$\text{mass} = n \times M = (0.0632 \text{ mol})(18.02 \text{ g/mol}) = 1.14 \text{ g}$$

**Answer:** $$\boxed{1.1 \text{ g (or 1.14 g)}}$$

### Part b) Compare moles of gaseous bromine to water

**Given Information:**
- Bromine (Br₂) has boiling point of 58.8°C
- Same container (2.0 L) and same conditions (99°C)
- Need to compare: moles of Br₂ vapor vs. moles of H₂O vapor

**Analysis:**

The vapor pressure at a given temperature depends on the strength of intermolecular forces.

**Water:** 
- Forms hydrogen bonds (very strong IMF)
- Lower vapor pressure at 99°C
- More molecules remain in liquid phase
- Fewer molecules in gas phase

**Bromine:**
- Nonpolar molecule, forms only London dispersion forces (weaker IMF)
- Weaker IMF than hydrogen bonds
- At 99°C, bromine will have a much higher vapor pressure than its normal boiling point
- Many more bromine molecules will be in the gas phase

**Explanation:**
Since bromine forms weaker intermolecular forces (only London dispersion forces) compared to water (hydrogen bonds), it evaporates more easily and has a higher vapor pressure at 99°C. Therefore, more moles of gaseous bromine will be present above the liquid compared to water in the same container.

**Answer:** $$\boxed{\text{The moles of Br}_2 \text{ would be GREATER than water}}$$

$$\boxed{\text{Reason: Br}_2 \text{ forms only weak London dispersion forces while water forms strong}}$$
$$\boxed{\text{hydrogen bonds, making Br}_2 \text{ more volatile and have higher vapor pressure}}$$

## **Question 4: Preparing a Solution of Copper (II) Sulfate Pentahydrate**

**Given Information:**
- Need to prepare: 50.0 mL of 0.20 M solution
- Solute: CuSO₄·5H₂O (copper (II) sulfate pentahydrate)
- Need to find: mass of CuSO₄·5H₂O required

**Step 1: Calculate molar mass of CuSO₄·5H₂O**

Cu: 63.55 g/mol
S: 32.07 g/mol
O (in SO₄): 4 × 16.00 = 64.00 g/mol
H₂O: 5 × 18.02 = 90.10 g/mol

Total molar mass = 63.55 + 32.07 + 64.00 + 90.10 = **249.72 g/mol**

**Step 2: Calculate moles needed using molarity formula**
$$M = \frac{\text{moles}}{\text{liters of solution}}$$

$$\text{moles} = M \times V = (0.20 \text{ mol/L})(0.050 \text{ L}) = 0.010 \text{ mol}$$

**Step 3: Convert moles to grams**
$$\text{mass} = \text{moles} \times \text{molar mass}$$

$$\text{mass} = (0.010 \text{ mol})(249.72 \text{ g/mol}) = 2.50 \text{ g}$$

**Answer:** $$\boxed{2.5 \text{ g of CuSO}_4\cdot 5\text{H}_2\text{O}}$$

## **Question 5: Preparing a Diluted HCl Solution**

**Given Information:**
- Starting solution: 0.50 M HCl
- Final solution needed: 250.0 mL of 0.20 M HCl
- Need to find: procedure with appropriate glassware

**Step 1: Use dilution formula to find volume needed**
$$M_1V_1 = M_2V_2$$

$$V_1 = \frac{M_2V_2}{M_1} = \frac{(0.20 \text{ M})(250.0 \text{ mL})}{0.50 \text{ M}} = 100.0 \text{ mL}$$

**Step 2: Describe the procedure**

The proper procedure for dilution using appropriate glassware:

**Procedure:**

1. **Measure the stock solution:** Use a buret or graduated cylinder to measure exactly 100.0 mL of the 0.50 M HCl solution.

2. **Add to volumetric flask:** Pour the measured 100.0 mL of concentrated HCl into a 250.0 mL volumetric flask.

3. **Dilute the solution:** Add enough deionized water to the flask until it is about 3/4 full. This helps dissipate the heat from the dilution process (mixing HCl with water is exothermic).

4. **Cool if necessary:** Let the solution cool to room temperature if warming occurred.

5. **Fill to the mark:** Add deionized water drop-by-drop using a dropper/pipette until the bottom of the meniscus is exactly at the 250.0 mL calibration mark on the volumetric flask.

6. **Mix thoroughly:** Stopper the flask and invert it several times (at least 10-15 times) to ensure complete mixing and homogeneity.

**Answer:** 
$$\boxed{\text{Measure 100.0 mL of 0.50 M HCl using a buret}}$$
$$\boxed{\text{Pour into a 250.0 mL volumetric flask}}$$
$$\boxed{\text{Add distilled water until reaching the 250.0 mL calibration mark}}$$
$$\boxed{\text{Stopper and shake/invert to mix thoroughly}}$$

## **Question 6: Dilution of Bleach Solution (Back-calculation)**

**Given Information:**
- Original diluted bleach: 5.0 mL
- Dilution volume: 100.0 mL (in volumetric flask)
- Titration result of diluted solution: 0.038 M
- Need to find: original molarity of bleach

**Step 1: Use dilution formula M₁V₁ = M₂V₂**

In this case:
- M₁ = original molarity of bleach (unknown)
- V₁ = original volume of bleach = 5.0 mL
- M₂ = molarity after dilution = 0.038 M
- V₂ = final volume after dilution = 100.0 mL

**Step 2: Solve for M₁**
$$M_1 = \frac{M_2V_2}{V_1} = \frac{(0.038 \text{ M})(100.0 \text{ mL})}{5.0 \text{ mL}}$$

$$M_1 = \frac{3.8}{5.0} = 0.76 \text{ M}$$

**Answer:** $$\boxed{0.76 \text{ M}}$$

### Part c) Effect of removing paper after 15 minutes instead of 30 minutes

**Analysis:**

In paper chromatography, separation depends on time because:
- The solvent (mobile phase) travels up the paper
- Different compounds travel at different rates depending on their polarity and interaction with the paper
- The longer the time, the greater the distance traveled by both compounds

**Prediction:**

If the paper is removed after **15 minutes instead of 30 minutes:**
- The solvent front will not travel as far up the paper
- Both compound X and compound Y will not travel as far
- The separation distance between X and Y will be **SMALLER/LESS** than in the 30-minute experiment

**Explanation:**

Since both compounds have less time to travel up the paper, they won't reach the distances they would have at 30 minutes. The distance traveled is proportional to time in chromatography, so removing the paper at half the time (15 min vs 30 min) results in roughly half the separation distance.

**Answer:** 
$$\boxed{\text{The separation distance between X and Y would DECREASE}}$$
$$\boxed{\text{Reason: With less time (15 min vs 30 min), the solvent front and compounds}}$$
$$\boxed{\text{travel shorter distances, reducing the separation between X and Y}}$$

### Part c) Explain solubility differences between dichloromethane and carbon tetrachloride

**Given Information:**
- Dichloromethane (CH₂Cl₂): greater solubility in water
- Carbon tetrachloride (CCl₄): less solubility in water
- Water is a polar solvent

**Analysis:**

**Dichloromethane (CH₂Cl₂):**
- Contains C-H and C-Cl bonds
- Due to different electronegativities of H and Cl, the molecule is POLAR
- Has a permanent dipole moment
- Polar molecules can form dipole-dipole attractions with water (polar)
- These dipole-dipole attractions are stronger than dipole-induced dipole attractions
- Can break some H-bonds in water and some dipole-dipole attractions within itself
- GREATER solubility in water

**Carbon Tetrachloride (CCl₄):**
- Contains only C-Cl bonds
- Symmetrical tetrahedral geometry cancels bond dipoles
- Results in a NONPOLAR molecule
- Can only form dipole-induced dipole attractions with polar water
- These are weak attractions
- Cannot effectively break H-bonds in water
- LESS solubility in water

**Answer:**

$$\boxed{\text{Dichloromethane is POLAR (due to different C-H and C-Cl bonds)}}$$
$$\boxed{\text{and forms strong dipole-dipole attractions with polar water.}}$$
$$\boxed{\text{Carbon tetrachloride is NONPOLAR (symmetric structure) and forms}}$$
$$\boxed{\text{only weak dipole-induced dipole attractions with water.}}$$
$$\boxed{\text{Since the dipole-dipole attractions between CH}_2\text{Cl}_2 \text{ and water are}}$$
$$\boxed{\text{stronger than dipole-induced dipole attractions, CH}_2\text{Cl}_2}$$
$$\boxed{\text{has greater solubility in water than CCl}_4\text{.}}$$

### Part d) Explain higher vapor pressure of dichloromethane vs. carbon tetrachloride

**Analysis:**

The question asks about vapor pressure at 20°C.

**Vapor Pressure Relationship:**
- Higher vapor pressure = weaker intermolecular forces
- Lower vapor pressure = stronger intermolecular forces

**From the data table:**
- Dichloromethane: vapor pressure = 353 mmHg (higher)
- Carbon tetrachloride: vapor pressure = 89 mmHg (lower)

**Intermolecular Forces:**

**Dichloromethane (CH₂Cl₂):**
- Polar molecule
- Forms dipole-dipole attractions
- Also forms London dispersion forces
- The dipole-dipole attractions are WEAKER than the London dispersion forces in CCl₄

**Carbon Tetrachloride (CCl₄):**
- Nonpolar molecule
- Forms only London dispersion forces
- However, CCl₄ has a LARGE electron cloud (4 chlorine atoms)
- Larger electron clouds are more polarizable
- More polarizable molecules form STRONGER London dispersion forces
- Stronger LDF's result in lower vapor pressure

**Answer:**

$$\boxed{\text{Although dichloromethane forms dipole-dipole attractions,}}$$
$$\boxed{\text{carbon tetrachloride forms STRONGER London dispersion forces}}$$
$$\boxed{\text{due to its larger, more polarizable electron cloud.}}$$
$$\boxed{\text{The strong LDF's in CCl}_4 \text{ require more energy to break,}}$$
$$\boxed{\text{resulting in lower vapor pressure. Conversely, the weaker}}$$
$$\boxed{\text{overall intermolecular attractions in CH}_2\text{Cl}_2 \text{ require less}}$$
$$\boxed{\text{energy to break, resulting in higher vapor pressure.}}$$
