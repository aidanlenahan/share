# PROBLEM 1: EQUILIBRIUM EXPRESSIONS

## Part A: PbCl₂(s) ⇌ Pb²⁺(aq) + 2Cl⁻(aq)

**Given Reaction:** Solubility equilibrium (heterogeneous)

**Key Concept:** When writing equilibrium expressions:
- Do NOT include solids (s)
- Do NOT include pure liquids (l)
- Only include gases (g) and aqueous species (aq)

**Writing the Expression:**
- Reactants: PbCl₂(s) - this is a SOLID, so we EXCLUDE it
- Products: Pb²⁺(aq) and 2Cl⁻(aq) - both are aqueous

**Form:** K = (products)/(reactants)

Since we have no aqueous/gaseous reactants in the expression:

$$K_{sp} = [Pb^{2+}][Cl^-]^2$$

**Note:** The coefficient "2" from 2Cl⁻ becomes an exponent on [Cl⁻]

---

$$\boxed{K_{sp} = [Pb^{2+}][Cl^-]^2}$$

---

## Part B: 2NO₂(g) ⇌ 2NO(g) + O₂(g)

**Given Reaction:** Gaseous equilibrium (all gases)

**Key Concept:** For gaseous equilibria, use Kₚ with partial pressures

**Writing the Expression:**
- All substances are gases, so we include all of them
- Products: 2NO(g) and O₂(g)
- Reactants: 2NO₂(g)

**Form:** K = (products)/(reactants) with exponents from coefficients

$$K_p = \frac{(P_{NO})^2 \times P_{O_2}}{(P_{NO_2})^2}$$

**Breaking it down:**
- Numerator: NO has coefficient 2, so (P_{NO})²; O₂ has coefficient 1, so P_{O₂}¹
- Denominator: NO₂ has coefficient 2, so (P_{NO₂})²

---

$$\boxed{K_p = \frac{(P_{NO})^2(P_{O_2})}{(P_{NO_2})^2}}$$

---

## Part C: 2KClO₃(s) ⇌ 2KCl(s) + 3O₂(g)

**Given Reaction:** Mixed equilibrium (solids and gas)

**Key Concept:** Only gaseous and aqueous species are included in K expressions

**Analysis of reactants and products:**
- Reactants: 2KClO₃(s) - this is a SOLID, EXCLUDE
- Products: 2KCl(s) - this is a SOLID, EXCLUDE
- Products: 3O₂(g) - this is a GAS, INCLUDE

**Writing the Expression:**
Since both solid reactants and solid products are excluded, only the gas remains:

For Kc: $$K_c = [O_2]^3$$

For Kp: $$K_p = (P_{O_2})^3$$

**Important Note:** For this reaction with only ONE gaseous substance:
- There is only ONE possible pressure/concentration value at equilibrium for a given temperature
- Different initial amounts won't create different equilibrium positions (as long as solid is present)

---

$$\boxed{K_c = [O_2]^3 \text{ or } K_p = (P_{O_2})^3}$$

# PROBLEM 2: CALCULATING NEW K VALUES

## Part A: What is K for 2A + 4B ⇌ 6C if A + 2B ⇌ 3C with K = 3 × 10²?

**Given Information:**
- Original reaction: A + 2B ⇌ 3C with K₁ = 3 × 10²
- Desired reaction: 2A + 4B ⇌ 6C
- Need to find: K_new

**Step 1: Analyze the relationship between reactions**

Compare the two reactions:
- Original: A + 2B ⇌ 3C
- Desired: 2A + 4B ⇌ 6C

The desired reaction is the original reaction **MULTIPLIED BY 2**

Desired = 2 × (Original reaction)

**Step 2: Apply the rule for multiplying reactions**

**Rule:** When a reaction is multiplied by a coefficient n, the new K = (K_old)ⁿ

In this case: n = 2

**Step 3: Calculate the new K**

$$K_{new} = (K_{old})^n$$

$$K_{new} = (3 \times 10^2)^2$$

$$K_{new} = 3^2 \times (10^2)^2$$

$$K_{new} = 9 \times 10^4$$

---

$$\boxed{K_{new} = 9 \times 10^4}$$

---

## Part B: What is K for 3C ⇌ 2B + A if A + 2B ⇌ 3C with K = 3 × 10²?

**Given Information:**
- Original reaction: A + 2B ⇌ 3C with K₁ = 3 × 10²
- Desired reaction: 3C ⇌ 2B + A
- Need to find: K_new

**Step 1: Analyze the relationship**

The desired reaction has:
- Reactants: 3C
- Products: 2B + A

The original reaction has:
- Reactants: A + 2B
- Products: 3C

The desired reaction is the **REVERSE** (reverse direction) of the original reaction!

**Step 2: Apply the rule for reverse reactions**

**Rule:** For the reverse reaction, K_reverse = 1/K_forward

**Step 3: Calculate**

$$K_{reverse} = \frac{1}{K_{forward}}$$

$$K_{new} = \frac{1}{3 \times 10^2}$$

$$K_{new} = \frac{1}{300}$$

$$K_{new} = 3.33... \times 10^{-3}$$

Or more precisely:

$$K_{new} = \frac{1}{3} \times 10^{-2} = 0.333... \times 10^{-2} = 3.33 \times 10^{-3}$$

---

$$\boxed{K_{new} = 3.3 \times 10^{-3}}$$

# PROBLEM 3B: LE CHATELIER'S PRINCIPLE TABLE

---

## Row 1: A is added

**Stress:** Adding more A(g) to the system at equilibrium

**What happens immediately:**
- We add more reactant A
- Q (reaction quotient) = [AB]/([A][B]) decreases because [A] in denominator increases
- Since Q < K, the system shifts RIGHT to re-establish equilibrium

**Direction of shift:** **RIGHT** (forward reaction speeds up temporarily)

**Effect on P_A:**
- When we ADD A, [A] increases immediately
- The rightward shift consumes some A, reducing [A]
- But the net effect is that [A] is still higher than before we added it
- Therefore: P_A goes **UP**

**Effect on K:**
- K only changes if temperature changes
- We didn't change temperature
- Therefore: K **DOESN'T CHANGE**

---

$$\boxed{\text{Shift: RIGHT} \quad \text{P}_A: \text{UP} \quad K: \text{NO CHANGE}}$$

---

**Interpretation of "shifts right":**
- The forward reaction is occurring faster than the reverse
- More solid is dissolving into solution
- The ionic concentrations in solution are increasing

- Solubility is the amount of solid that dissolves
- If the reaction shifts right, more solid is dissolving
- Solubility is INCREASING

---

$$\boxed{\text{Shifting right = DISSOLVING (increasing solubility)}}$$

---

**Interpretation of "shifts left":**
- The reverse reaction is occurring faster than the forward
- Solution ions are combining to reform solid
- Ionic concentrations in solution are decreasing

**What is happening to solubility?**
- If the reaction shifts left, ions in solution are reforming solid
- Less dissolved solid remains in solution
- Solubility is DECREASING

---

$$\boxed{\text{Shifting left = PRECIPITATING (crystallizing, decreasing solubility)}}$$

# PROBLEM 6: N₂O₄/NO₂ EQUILIBRIUM AT 25°C

**Reaction:** N₂O₄(g) ⇌ 2NO₂(g)

---

## Given Information

| Variable | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Temperature | 25°C |
| Kₚ | 0.15 |
| P_{N₂O₄} at equilibrium | 0.85 atm |
| Find | P_{NO₂} at equilibrium |

---

## Solution

**Step 1: Write the equilibrium expression for Kp**

For the reaction: N₂O₄(g) ⇌ 2NO₂(g)

Products in numerator, reactants in denominator, with exponents from coefficients:

$$K_p = \frac{(P_{NO_2})^2}{P_{N_2O_4}}$$

---

**Step 2: Substitute known values**

$$0.15 = \frac{(P_{NO_2})^2}{0.85}$$

---

**Step 3: Solve for (P_{NO₂})²**

Multiply both sides by 0.85:

$$(P_{NO_2})^2 = 0.15 \times 0.85$$

$$(P_{NO_2})^2 = 0.1275$$

---

**Step 4: Take the square root**

$$P_{NO_2} = \sqrt{0.1275}$$

$$P_{NO_2} = 0.357 \text{ atm}$$

---

## Verification

Check by substituting back into Kₚ:

$$K_p = \frac{(0.357)^2}{0.85} = \frac{0.1274}{0.85} = 0.150$$

## Part 16c: Effect of adding CO₂ to raise pressure to 1.5 atm

### Question

After reaching equilibrium at 1.04 atm (at 20 minutes), some CO₂(g) is injected to raise the pressure to 1.5 atm.

What would the final pressure be?

---

### Step 1: Analyze the disturbance

**Before injection:**
- System was at equilibrium
- P_{CO₂} = 1.04 atm
- This equals Kp for this reaction at 1100 K

**After injection:**
- Additional CO₂ added
- P_{CO₂} increases to 1.5 atm
- Q = P_{CO₂} > Kp (1.5 > 1.04)
- System is NO LONGER at equilibrium!

---

### Step 2: Apply Le Chatelier's principle

Since Q > Kp, the reaction must shift LEFT (reverse direction) to consume some CO₂ and return to equilibrium.

**Reverse reaction:** CaO(s) + CO₂(g) → CaCO₃(s)

---

### Step 3: Determine final pressure

The system will shift LEFT until Q = Kp again.

Since Kp for this reaction is determined solely by the gas pressure (solids don't appear in the expression), and the temperature hasn't changed...

**The equilibrium pressure MUST return to 1.04 atm**

The extra CO₂ will react with CaO to form CaCO₃(s), reducing the CO₂ pressure back to the equilibrium value.

---

$$\boxed{\text{The final pressure would be EQUAL TO 1.04 atm}}$$

$$\boxed{\text{Explanation: The equilibrium pressure at 1100 K is determined by Kp.}}$$
$$\boxed{\text{When CO}_2 \text{ is added (Q > Kp), the reverse reaction shifts until}}$$
$$\boxed{\text{P}_{CO_2} \text{ returns to 1.04 atm.}}$$
