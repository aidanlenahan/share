## **PROBLEM 1**

### **1a.) Final Temperature of Gold**

**Given Information:**
- Mass of gold = 192.4 g
- Initial temperature (T₁) = 30.0°C
- Heat absorbed (q) = 1226 cal
- Specific heat of gold (C) = 0.03085 cal/g·°C

**Formula:**
$$q = m \times C \times \Delta T$$

Where: $\Delta T = T_f - T_i$

**Rearranging for final temperature:**
$$\Delta T = \frac{q}{m \times C}$$

$$T_f = T_i + \Delta T$$

**Step 1: Calculate ΔT**
$$\Delta T = \frac{1226 \text{ cal}}{192.4 \text{ g} \times 0.03085 \text{ cal/g·°C}}$$

$$\Delta T = \frac{1226}{5.928} = 206.86°C$$

**Step 2: Calculate final temperature**
$$T_f = 30.0°C + 206.86°C = 236.86°C$$

$$\boxed{T_f = 236.55°C \text{ (or 237°C)}}$$

**Check with Answer Key:** ✓ Correct (Answer key shows 236.55°C)

---

### **1b.) Effect of Lower Specific Heat**

**Analysis:**

Using the same equation: $\Delta T = \frac{q}{m \times C}$

If we absorb the same amount of heat (1226 cal) but use a metal with a **lower specific heat (smaller C value)**:

- The denominator $(m \times C)$ becomes smaller
- Therefore, ΔT becomes **larger**
- Since $T_f = T_i + \Delta T$, a larger ΔT means a higher final temperature

**Explanation:** Substances with lower specific heat require less energy to raise their temperature by 1°C. Therefore, the same amount of heat will produce a greater temperature change.

$$\boxed{\text{The final temperature would be HIGHER}}$$

**Check with Answer Key:** ✓ Correct (Answer key confirms: higher)

Reactants:

### **2b.) Using Bond Enthalpies**

**Formula:**
$$\Delta H_{rxn}° = \text{(Energy to break bonds)} - \text{(Energy released making bonds)}$$
$$\Delta H_{rxn}° = \sum BE(\text{reactants}) - \sum BE(\text{products})$$

- C=C: 607 kJ/mol
- C-H: 413 kJ/mol
- O=O: 499 kJ/mol

$$\boxed{\Delta H_{comb}° \approx -1307 \text{ kJ/mol}}$$

## **PROBLEM 5**

### **Particle-Level Reasoning for Problem 4E**

**Reaction:** NH₄⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq) → NH₃(g) + H₂O(l)

**Explanation:**

In the reactants, we have two ions dissolved in aqueous solution. While aqueous ions have some disorder due to hydration shells and movement in solution, they are confined to the liquid phase and interact with water molecules.

In the products, we form ammonia gas and liquid water. The formation of a gas is the key driving factor here.

**Gas phase particles have significantly more available microstates than ions in solution because:**

1. **Greater spatial freedom**: Gas molecules can occupy any position in a container with essentially no restrictions, whereas aqueous ions are constrained to the solution volume and must maintain hydration shells.

2. **More translational motion**: Gas particles move freely in three dimensions with a wide range of velocities, creating many more possible configurations (microstates).

3. **Vibrational and rotational freedom**: Gas phase molecules have accessible vibrational and rotational energy levels that add to the number of available microstates.

4. **No intermolecular constraints**: Unlike aqueous ions that experience electrostatic attractions, hydrogen bonding (with water), and other interactions in solution, gas molecules are largely independent.

Even though we also form liquid water (which has lower entropy than the ions), the **formation of the gas phase dominates the entropy change and causes ΔS° to be positive**.

$$\boxed{\text{The gas phase formed has far more available microstates}}$$
$$\boxed{\text{than aqueous ions, making ΔS° positive}}$$

**Check with Answer Key:** ✓ Correct

### **6i.) Under What Conditions is This Reaction Spontaneous?**

**Analysis:**

Using the equation: $\Delta G° = \Delta H° - T\Delta S°$

With ΔH° = negative and ΔS° = negative:

At **low temperature:**
- The term $-T\Delta S°$ becomes small in magnitude (small negative number becomes larger positive number, but multiplied by negative ΔS° gives smaller positive value)
- Actually, since ΔS° is negative: $-T\Delta S° = -(T) \times (-\text{small}) = \text{positive small}$
- At low T: ΔG° = (large negative) - (small positive) = **negative** ✓ Spontaneous

At **high temperature:**
- The term $-T\Delta S°$ becomes large in magnitude (large value × negative ΔS°)
- Since ΔS° is negative: $-T\Delta S° = -(T) \times (-\text{large}) = \text{larger positive}$
- At high T: ΔG° = (negative) - (large positive) = **positive** ✗ Nonspontaneous

Therefore, this reaction is spontaneous at **low temperatures**.

$$\boxed{\text{Circle: LOW TEMPERATURE}}$$

### **6j.) What is K?**

**Formula relating ΔG° and K:**
$$\Delta G° = -RT \ln(K)$$

Where:
- R = 8.314 J/mol·K = 0.008314 kJ/mol·K
- T = 298 K
- ΔG° = -549,000 J/mol (convert to J first)

**Rearranging for K:**
$$\ln(K) = -\frac{\Delta G°}{RT}$$

$$\ln(K) = -\frac{(-549,000)}{(8.314)(298)}$$

$$\ln(K) = \frac{549,000}{2,477.6} = 221.6$$

$$K = e^{221.6} = 3.85 \times 10^{96}$$

$$\boxed{K = 3.85 \times 10^{96}}$$
